Entropy

We already know that random numbers are valuable and essential in computing, especially in cybersecurity. However, a computer is a machine whose operation we program and from which we expect a deterministic behavior. Random number generation is a non-deterministic task. So, how do we translate this task to the computer? Is it really possible?

The concept of entropy in physics is generally associated with the measurement of the amount of disorder or chaos in a system. Natural sources of entropy can be found in the flow of water in a river, ambient noise in decibels, temperature variations, etc.

However, in a computer, there may be other sources of noise that can constantly provide us with purely random values, such as keyboard strokes, mouse movement, network flow, current electricity, sound captured by the microphone... This is what we call entropy. The more noise, the more entropy, and therefore, the less predictable.

Randomness is so important that operating systems have their own mechanisms to manage entropy. For example, in Linux, obtained entropy is stored in the files /dev/random and /dev/urandom. The difference between these two files lies in the entropy check: while a request to /dev/urandom will return a value even if there is not much entropy in the source, /dev/random will check first if the amount of entropy in the system is sufficient to reach the specified minimum and will block the request until that minimum is satisfied.

From these sources of entropy, we can obtain purely random numbers. However, this can be a costly process, and the flow of random numbers we need may be greater than the entropy the computer can gather, leading to blocking.

Therefore, to fulfill the mission of generating random numbers on a computer securely and efficiently, starting from an initially purely random number (seed), we can continue generating a sequence of pseudo-random numbers from such a seed using a pseudo-random number generator (PRNG).
